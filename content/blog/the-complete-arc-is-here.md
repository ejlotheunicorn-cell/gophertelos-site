When I started building the Gopher Telos Engine, I wasn't building a curriculum. I was building one year — the year my son Curtis needed — at our kitchen table, because nothing we could buy was reaching him.

Today the whole thing is finished. **Pre-K through Grade 6. All seven levels. The complete arc.**

I want to say clearly what that means, because it's the part that matters most: this was never seven separate products. It's one system, sequenced from beginning to end, and now you can walk a reader through the entire journey without ever leaving it.

## What "one system" actually means

Every grade in the Engine keeps the same spine — the 3×7 Syntactic Matrix. Three sentence tiers, Simple, Compound, and Complex, across seven rows in every lesson. That structure never changes. What changes, one controlled step at a time, is how much of English's irregularity a reader has to carry.

Here is the arc, start to finish:

- **Pre-K / K** — Every word is CVC. One letter, one sound. The safe, shallow water where decoding confidence is built before anything gets hard.
- **Grade 1** — The first blends and silent-e words. Still shallow, but the gears start turning.
- **Grade 2** — Vowel teams and r-controlled vowels. The first real layer of English's unpredictability, introduced gently.
- **Grade 3** — Multisyllabic words, prefixes and suffixes. Readers start taking on real length.
- **Grade 4** — The deep water begins. Abstract nouns, Latin and Greek roots, subordinate clauses.
- **Grade 5** — Embedded relative clauses, derivational morphology. The matrix becomes a diagnostic tool the reader uses on any sentence.
- **Grade 6** — Full academic English. Advanced roots, academic vocabulary, complex sentences across all three tiers. The engine is internalized.

A child who starts at "The cat sat" and ends at mapping the structure of an academic paragraph has never switched systems. They've climbed one staircase — the same handrail the whole way up.

## Why I built it this way

Struggling and dyslexic readers do best when English is introduced from its most reliable patterns outward. Start transparent, add irregularity one controlled step at a time, and keep real sentence structure at the center of every lesson — never a nonsense drill string. That principle holds identical from Pre-K to Grade 6. The only thing that deepens is the water.

I didn't know when I started whether the idea would hold across a whole K–6 span. It did. The same structure that got Curtis through his first decodable sentence is the structure that carries a sixth grader through a complex, multi-clause academic one.
